Introduction

This project was inspired by personal interest. I was curious how Hennikers property taxes compare to other NH communities. My interest continued to grow as voting season revved up, my December property tax bill arrived, and talks of continued inflation painted the media. Rather than accepting baseless claims at face value, I wanted to take the time to research and verify Hennikers property tax information for myself. I aimed to provide context by combining data from several different sources. Some of my findings were as expected, and others came by surprise.

 

This analysis focuses on exploring four main questions:

1)       How is Hennikers current (2024) tax rate and total valuation positioned comparatively to NH towns of similar population, geographics, and economic opportunity?

2)       How has the property tax burden in Henniker changed over time relative to consumer price and wage inflation?

3)       How have the components that make up Hennikers total tax rate changed over time?

4)       Could trends in Hennikers age distribution have warranted changes in how tax components have been allocated over time?

 

 

 

About the Data

This analysis is based on data gathered from several town, state, and federal sources. I believe these sources to be reasonably accurate but its important to remember that data can be subject to interpretation, and there may be nuances or variations depending on how and when the information was collected and recorded from each source.

Information on tax rates and valuations from 1993-2024 was sourced from the New Hampshire Department of Revenue. Information used to compare populations of towns was taken from the New Hampshire Office of Planning and Development (NH OPD). Historical Henniker population data was collected from Henniker Annual Reports using the Economic and Community Profile from 2008-2023. The US Census was used for the years 2000 and 1990, and a regression analysis imputation was calculated and applied to remaining years where sourced population data was not available. Data collected on price inflation and wage inflation was taken from the US Department of Labor Statistics. Overall, the data collected spans 32 years from 1993-2024. Population analysis is primarily focused on a smaller range of roughly 16 years, from 2008-2024. This was due to the inability to source earlier data relating to age segmentation in Hennikers population.

Primary variables include the year, total tax rate, each component of the total tax rate, valuation without utilities, estimated population, median NH income, and Northeast CPI. Using these variables, I engineered additional variables creating several percentage changes over time, or lag variables, as well as a normalized index for price inflation, wage inflation, and tax burden variables. Here is a more detailed description of each data source:

 

Tax Rate and Valuation Data

Information on all NH town and city tax rates and valuations from 1993-2024 was sourced from the New Hampshire Department of Revenue. This data includes annualized historical property tax data for each NH town and city. The primary variables used in the analysis include the date, town, county, total tax rate, each component of the tax rate, and valuation without utilities. Several of these variables were used for feature engineering. This includes the date variable being used to create the tax_yr variable. Additionally, lag variables in which the percentage change on an annual basis for several variables were computed and incorporated into the analysis. The proportions of tax components relative to the total tax rate were also engineered and applied to the analysis. The tax_burden variable was engineered by dividing validations without utilities by one-thousand and then multiplying the result by the total tax rate in that year. The tax_burden approximates how much tax revenues were collected based on valuation without utilities and tax rate. Its important to note that this figure is a reasonable estimate and that uncollected taxes, exceptions, and credits other than non-utility exceptions are likely to result in nominal differences in actual property tax revenues. All information obtained from the source regarding unincorporated NH towns has been removed from this dataset.

 

Historical Population Data all NH towns

Information used to compare populations of NH towns and cities was taken from the New Hampshire Office of Planning and Development (NH OPD). The variables obtained and used in the analysis include the town and its corresponding estimated 2023 population. This population data was used only to determine what New Hampshire towns and cities make good population benchmarks for Henniker. It also assumes that no drastic population changes in towns used occurred from 2023 to 2024. This data was not used in evaluating Hennikers population overtime.

 

Henniker Population Data

Data used in evaluating Hennikers population overtime was taken from Henniker Annual Town Reports in the Economic and Community Profile section from 2008-2023. US Census population data was incorporated into this historical data for the year 2000 and 1990. A regression analysis imputation was then calculated and applied to remaining years where sourced data on Hennikers population was not available.

 

Consumer Price Data

Data collected on price inflation was gathered from the US Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS). Historical records of the Northeast Consumer Price Index All-Items Report were utilized to gather the consumer price inflation information used in the analysis.

 

Wage Inflation Data

Wage Inflation was also collected from the US Bureau of Labor Statistics. Historical Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics Reports were utilized. The primary variable extracted from these reports was the median annual income. NH was isolated and the median annual wage from each sector was averaged to create a median NH wage reflecting all sectors.

 

Additional Engineered Data

As noted in some of the examples above, feature engineering was vital to bringing context into the analysis. In addition to the examples of feature engineering already mentioned, merging sources created new opportunities. For example, the per capita tax burden was engineered using estimated population and tax burden data. Tax burden, cpi, and wage data indexed variables were created by normalizing each variable to the same scale. This allowed the trends in cpi, wages, and tax burdens to be compared directly to one another.


 

 

NH Property Tax Background

In New Hampshire, property taxes are a primary source of revenue for local governments and services. These taxes are levied on real property (like land and buildings) and are calculated based on assessed values of properties. Property tax rates in New Hampshire are expressed as a dollar amount per $1,000 of assessed value. For example, if a property has an assessed value of $100,000 and the property tax rate in the town is $25.00, the total annual property taxes due will be $2,500. This method is informally known as the "three-finger rule."

Additionally, the combined assessed values of all taxable real properties within a town or city are summed to form the communitys total valuation, which serves as the basis for overall tax calculations. Because utilities are often taxed under different rules or at different rates, many municipalities exclude these properties from the primary valuation used to set local tax rates. This approach ensures that the tax rate accurately reflects the value of properties directly contributing to municipal services and revenue.

There are four segmented components that make up the total property tax rate. They are the municipal tax, county tax, local education tax, and state education tax.

Municipal Tax (Town Services Tax):

This is the tax rate is levied by the town to fund services like fire, police, town highway departments, parks, and other town services. The municipal tax rate directly funds the services that the town provides to residents.

County Tax:

Counties in New Hampshire also impose property taxes. These taxes fund county-wide services like the county court system, county jail, and other further reaching public services.

Local Education Tax (School District Tax):

Towns and cities levy local education taxes to adequately fund public schools, teacher salaries, maintenance of facilities, educational programs, and other educational initiatives within their school district.

State Education Tax:

The state education tax rate is a mandatory statewide property tax that helps fund the public school system. All municipalities are required to levy the state education tax rate on their property tax bills. This tax is used to provide equalized funding for schools across New Hampshire, ensuring that all districts have a baseline level of funding regardless of local property values.

Section 1: 2024 Henniker Tax Rate and Valuation

Henniker Baseline

Henniker's tax rate was $23.90 per $1,000 in accessed value in 2024. Total 2024 town valuations without utilities equaled $734,404,799. Both figures are critical in determining the property tax burden for individual owners and the overall revenue generated by the town. As with all NH towns and cities, Henniker property taxes overwhelmingly make up the majority of revenue generated by the town.

 

Henniker vs All Incorporated NH Towns and Cities


In comparison to all incorporated towns in NH, the total 2024 Henniker tax rate is slightly above the third quantile. This means that in 2024, Henniker had a higher total tax rate than most other incorporated towns in NH. Henniker's property valuations (without utilities) are lower than the average but higher than the median total valuations for all incorporated towns in NH.

Given New Hampshire's unique tax structure which is heavily reliant on property tax valuation, comparing Henniker to all towns and cities in New Hampshire isn't appropriate or particularly useful. This is obvious when we consider an over 16-million-dollar range in valuations and a $30.97 range in total property tax rate throughout all incorporated NH towns and cities. Just like any sensible person wouldn't use Vail as a benchmark for Pat's Peak, comparing Henniker to Manchester or Rye doesn't provide us with useful or actionable information. We need to find better comparisons.

 

 

 

Henniker vs Merrimack County


By filtering data to include only towns and cities located in Merrimack County, we see improvement in Hennikers tax rate position. This improvement could be attributed to more closely aligned geographic and economic similarities. In Merrimack County, Henniker remains below average and above the median in total valuations. Hennikers tax rate moves to a more favorable level, falling within the middle 50% of all Merrimack County NH towns and cities. However, Henniker is above both the mean and median property tax rate for all incorporated towns and cities in Merrimack County.

I think we can do better. Looking at only Merrimack County is a comparative improvement, but it draws hard lines at close-by towns which may be some of the most comparable in terms of geography and economic accessibility. We're also failing to consider the size of the tax base which has a known relationship with total valuation. Ask yourself if it makes sense to compare Henniker to Concord? They are both in Merrimack County, but Concord has a more diverse revenue stream driven by increased business activity, a larger population, and a broader tax base. These factors can significantly enhance its financial capacity and support a wider range of public services. Henniker is also uniquely situated in close proximity to three other counties. A better approach may be to consider all towns in Merrimack County and the three other surrounding counties. We can also set population parameters using data on town and city populations. This should facilitate better comparisons.

 

Henniker vs Benchmark Communities

By comparing Henniker to other NH towns with similar geographic, economic, and population characteristics, we can get a more accurate and equitable comparison. I use the NH Office of Planning and Development (OPD) 2023 estimated population to see which towns and cities have a similar population to Henniker. Hennikers 2023 OPD estimated population is 4,585. I also limit counties to Merrimack, Hillsborough, Sullivan, and Cheshire counties. Henniker is located close to the center of these four counties. Many of the towns and cities found within these four counties share similar geographic and economic conditions. The results uncover fifteen comparable towns with populations between 3,500 and 5,500 located in Merrimack, Hillsborough, Sullivan, and Cheshire counties. I assume that populations in these 15 towns didn't suffer drastic change from 2023 to 2024. Let's
take a closer look at the populations in our new benchmark sample.

 

 

 


In review of summary statistics for tax rates and valuations without utilities in our benchmark sample, the lowest rate is found in New London, NH at $11.61 per $1,000 in accessed value. The highest 2024 tax rate belongs to the town of Charlestown at $36.36 per $1,000 in accessed value. The average tax rate falls very close to the center of this range at $23.78 per $1,000 in accessed value. Let's use a boxplot to take a closer look at how Henniker stacks up in our new benchmark sample.

 

Boxplot of Tax Rate


This boxplot helps visualize where the 2024 Henniker tax rate falls within the sample of 15 similar towns. We can see that the 2024 Henniker tax rate falls within the middle 50% of the data. It is close to centered within that 50% and is less than the median tax rate for the sample.

 

I want to emphasize again the importance of considering a towns total valuation when making tax rate comparisons. In New Hampshire, towns with high property values dont need high tax rates to bring in the same amount of money as towns with lower property values. That's why two neighboring towns, especially when one has a lot of waterfront property, can have very different tax rates.

 

Boxplot of Valuation

The boxplot below shows where Henniker valuations stack up in our benchmark sample. We see that there is a very noticeable outlier where property valuation is substantially higher than the other 14 towns in the benchmark sample. The outlier represents New London, NH. New London borders the renown Lake Sunapee and is also home to Little Lake Sunapee, Pleasant Lake, and a few other lesser-known lakes. Waterfront property in New London is abundant and the towns high total valuation reflects that. Although New London shares many similarities with the towns in our sample, its multi-million-dollar lakefront properties make it incomparable in terms of valuation and property tax rate. You may remember that New London also had the lowest tax rate in our sample. Given that they have the highest total property valuations, they can generate the same or more property tax revenue using a lower tax rate.


This is a perfect example of the imbalances that exist in New Hampshire's property reliant tax structure. In 2024, if you lived 25 minutes north of Henniker in the town of New London and lived in home accessed at $400,000, not only would you save almost $5,000 annually on property taxes, but you would also have better funded schools, fire, police, roads, parks, and other tax funded services.

 

Since Henniker doesn't attract the multi-million-dollar real estate market that New London does, it makes sense to remove New London from the sample concluding that it is not appropriate to compare to Henniker. Let's see how the removal of New London affects our total tax rate and total valuation.

 

 

 

Boxplot of tax rate - New London Removed

We can see that the removal of New London has not improved our total tax rate position substantially. However, the removal of the outlier has repositioned the Henniker tax rate below both the median ($25.14) and mean ($24.65) in the revised benchmark sample.

 

Boxplot of Valuations - New London Removed

Comparatively, we can see that Henniker valuations are higher than the majority of the towns in our revised sample. Given what we know, or just learned, about New Hampshire tax rates relationship with validations, it makes sense that our tax rate is lower than the benchmark sample average since our valuations are higher than the benchmark sample average. However, it's important to recognize that the balance is disproportionate. Hennikers tax rate is only slightly lower, but its valuations are substantially higher.

 

In our previous exploration, New London had the highest valuation and the lowest tax rate in the sample. Although that was the case, New Londons lower tax rate was also disproportionate with their valuations. Simply consider that their tax rate wasn't an outlier, but their valuations were. This suggests that their tax rate is high relative to their total valuations. In other words, New London is raising considerably more revenue per capita for roughly the same tax funding needs, and theyre doing it at a lower tax rate.

Comparatively, Henniker has the second highest valuation in the revised benchmark sample but only the 6th lowest tax rate in the sample. Henniker is also generating more revenue at a lower tax rate just not to the same extent as New London.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Section 2: Historical Trends in Hennikers Property Tax Rate, Valuations, and Taxpayer Burden

Percentage Change in Tax Rate and Valuation Over Time


This plot tracks the percentage change in the tax rate and the percentage change in property valuations over time. The diamond shapes that form in the plot more often than not reflect property reassessments. During property re-assessment years, total town valuations increase or decrease due to the reassessment of property values. Tax rates often follow suite by decreasing or increasing to combat these changes. This stabilizes town revenues and property taxpayer burden during reassessment years. When reassessments occur and property values increase, the same amount of money can be collected by the town at a lower tax rate. When valuations decrease, higher tax rates are needed for the town to collect the same amount of revenue.

 

With the exception of several re-assessment years, valuations are largely stable. However, the property tax rate is more erratic. This makes sense considering that our form of government allows for annual changes in the property tax rate but not in valuation. In years where the diamond shapes do not form, most notably in 1999 or 2008, tax rate changes either increase or decrease the burden on property taxpayers. 1999 could be viewed as a rate-driven tax break that lessened the burden on taxpayers. 2008 is an example of a rate-driven hike, placing more burden on taxpayers.

 

Additionally, if we look closer at the diamond shaped years, we can see that the percentage drop in either tax rate or valuation is not equal to the counter action increase in tax rate or valuation. For example, In 2005 valuations increased by over 100% while tax rates dropped by less than 50%. This results in a value-driven increase, putting more tax burden on property taxpayers through increased valuations. Overall, an increase in tax burden has been more common than a decrease in tax burden in Henniker from 1993 to 2024.

 

Tax Burden Over Time


By dividing validation records (without utilities) by one thousand and then multiplying the result by each years effective tax rate, we can determine how much revenue the town positioned itself to generate from property taxes each year, or the tax burden on property taxpayers. It's necessary to understand that this calculation is a simplified representation and does not consider special exceptions such as Henniker's elderly, disabled, or veteran related property tax discounts.

This plot gives us a visual representation of Henniker property tax revenues collected over time. It's clear that property tax generated revenues have increased from 1993 to 2024. In 1993, the town generated about 5 million in property tax revenues. In 2024, that same revenue stream generates roughly 17.5 million in revenues.

At first glance this may seem concerning, but we have to consider the underlying factors that lead to these increases or decreases in revenue over time. Some of the most relevant factors related to these increases or decreases include changes in town population and changes in inflation. Next, we'll consider how these two underlying factors compare to the increase in Hennikers property tax revenue generation since 1993.

 

 

CIP vs Per Capita Tax Burden over time

By calculating the year-over-year changes in Henniker's property valuations and tax rates, we can compare shifts in the property tax burden relative to population growth and inflation. This approach helps illustrate how tax burden has evolved over time in the context of the broader economic picture.


To account for population changes, I calculated the per capita property tax burden for each year. Additionally, I normalized both the consumer price index (CPI) and the per capita tax burden to a base value of 100, starting from 1993. This normalization ensures that per capita tax burden and inflation are directly comparable on the same scale.

 

We can see that from 1993 to 1998, the per capita tax burden increased faster than inflation. In 1999, the per capita tax burden sharply decreased before equalizing again in 2000. The per capita property tax burden then increased faster than inflation for the next 18 years, with the largest difference in per capita tax burden and inflation being felt in 2011, 2017 and in 2019. In 2020, the per capita tax burden receded. Most recently in 2023 and in 2024, the per capita tax burden is rising again faster than inflation.

When the per capita tax burden rises faster than inflation, it places more pressure on property owners by forcing them to pay a larger portion of their income toward taxes. For example, a Henniker resident in 1994 paid about as much of their income toward property taxes as a resident in 2022. In 2011, 2017 and in 2019, residents paid a much larger share of their income toward property taxes. Overall, the per capita tax burden in Henniker has outpaced inflation.

Although this plot provides some great insights through added context, in order to truly understand taxpayer burden, we must consider wage inflation. Thus far, we've assumed that taxpayer wages (private or public) have increased at the same pace as consumer price inflation.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

CPI, tax burden, and wage inflation over time

In this plot, I've normalized New Hampshire annual median wage inflation data from 1993 to 2024. Given limitations on the availability of wage data from 1993-1996 and in 2024, I used a regression imputation to estimate wage data for these first 5 years. This plot allows us to visualize how the Henniker property tax burden compares to trends in Northeast CPI inflation and in New Hampshire wage inflation.

 


 

To my surprise, wage growth in New Hampshire has outpaced inflation, generally increasing NH residents spending power since 1993. The plot shows this through the widening gap between the Northeast CPI line plot (red) and the NH Median Wage line plot (green).

However, the property tax burden in Henniker has often eaten up much or all of that increased spending power. This is visualized by the Per Capita Valuation + Tax Effect line plot (blue) in relation to NH Median Wage line plot (green). We can see that in some years, like in 2000, spending power increased. In other years, like 2010, it decreased after factoring in property tax burden. More recently, since 2020, spending power has been improving, as wage growth has outpaced the tax burden and the Northeast CPI has remained lower than the tax burden.

This current balance of property tax burden falling in-between wage inflation and CPI inflation could be considered ideal. It allows for investment in town services at a level above inflation (actual growth), while simultaneously ensuring property taxpayers have more disposable income.

These findings seem to contradict some recent narratives, so I believe its important to clarify a few points before moving on. First, by using the median wage, weve filtered out extreme outliers like CEO pay, which can skew data. Second, wages are not a measure of wealth or net worth. Many wealthy business owners and retirees, in an effort to minimize federal income taxes, often pay themselves the smallest salary they can live on. Since their homes, cars, and other assets may already be paid for, they can maintain a high standard of living while paying little or no federal income tax. These 'non-storytelling' salaries are included in our wage data. Third, because were considering all industries, its important to note that some sectors may have experienced significant declines, while others have seen substantial gains. Net wage growth doesn't account for industry-specific changes. In sectors like manufacturing and retail, wages may have lagged behind inflation, while in industries like technology and healthcare, wages may have grown much faster than inflation. Overall median wage growth doesn't capture these disparities across different sectors.

Similarly, not all CPI segments have grown or declined equally. While costs for insurance and healthcare have skyrocketed, prices for consumer goods may have stagnated or even declined in some years. This variation within the CPI index itself means that general inflation measures don't necessarily reflect the cost of living for all households equally.

Instead of diving deeper into specifics related to NH wages and Northeast CPI inflation, let's take a closer look at how Henniker property tax dollars are historically being allocated within our town.

 

 

 

 

 

Section 3: Henniker Tax Rate Components

Henniker Tax Rate Components over Time

This plot helps us to understand how each component of our tax rate has contributed to the total property tax rate over time. One of the most noticeable visualizations is the absence of the State Education tax from 1993-1999. Prior to 1999, New Hampshire did not have a state education tax component and relied solely on the local education tax component to fund public schools. Interestingly, a higher percentage of tax dollars were still being spent on schooling before the state school tax component was introduced.


 

The green coloring shows a steady decline in state education taxes as a percentage of the total tax. In fact, from its inception, the portion of total taxes being spent on state education has declined. As of 2024, state education tax has the lowest burden on Henniker property taxes.

That being said, this plot makes it clear that the local education tax component of property taxes in Henniker is the largest portion of property tax bills. However, upon close inspection, youll notice that from 1996 to 2022, the percentage of the total property tax being allocated to schools has shrunk from about 75% in 1995 to about 54% in 2022. For almost three decades education funding in Henniker as a percentage of the total property tax bill has declined. In 2023 and 2024 portions of local education as a percentage of the total are increasing again. It will be interesting to see if these 2023 and 2024 increases start to reverse the trend or if 2023 and 2024 are simple fluctuations in the long-term downward trend in Hennikers investment in public schools and in children.

The blue coloring represents the municipal tax component as a portion of the total property tax bill in Henniker. It shows a long-term upward trend. The municipal percentage of the total tax rate has been growing over the past three decades. This trend suggests that overtime, a larger percentage of town revenues are being allocated to town services such as fire, police, highway department, parks, and other town services and projects.

The county tax rate as a portion of the total has mostly been consistent. There have been some small fluctuations in the county tax rate as a percentage of the total tax rate, but not noteworthy in comparison to the other three property tax rate components.

Section 4: A Closer Look at Hennikers Population

Some justification could be made for school and non-school historical funding trends. For example, if our town has seen significant population growth in older age groups and significant population declines in younger age groups. Generally speaking, younger age groups and families benefit directly from the public school system, while older age groups and childless households may be less likely to benefit directly from the public school system.

Estimated population data is available for Henniker throughout the 1993-2024 timeline. However, the population by age data is more limited. Age specific data has only been captured since 2005. We can gain a general impression about population age trends in Henniker, but we cannot directly make comparisons dating from 1993 to 2005. Let's start by looking at the general population growth in Henniker since 1993.

 


Hennikers Population Over Time

 

Here, we can see an increase from about 4000 residents in 1993 to about 6500 in 2024. Population grew from 1993 to 2006. In 2007, it declined slightly until 2013. In 2014 the Henniker population began growing again with a large spike in population growth felt in 2020.

 

Henniker Population by Age (2009-2024)


This plot shows the change in age groups from 2009 to 2024 in Henniker. We can see that the under 5 age group has been mostly steady with small gains in population since 2009. The age group 55-65 years old saw a quick increase from 2009-2010 but then steadied to small gains from 2010-2024. Age groups 65 and older and 20-35 years old, considered the least likely to benefit directly from public schools, saw small declines from 2009-2016 and then substantial growth from 2015-2024. Age groups 5-19 and 35-54, considered the most likely to directly benefit from public schools, both have declined since 2009 despite recently experiencing increases.

 

 

Overall, the declines in age groups 5-19 and 35-45 may suggest that Henniker has been losing families as a percentage of its total population. Large increases in the 65 and older age group also suggest an aging population trend in Henniker. This information lends itself to some generalizations we've all heard about regarding NH state population trends. New Hampshire's tax structure and economy lends itself to retirees and an older population. With a large swath of the population in or nearing retirement age, New Hampshire has grown in population and has also grown older in population. Henniker seems aligned with this widespread NH generalization.

Group based Generalization of Child Homes and Childless Homes

Looking at age group trends and trying to compare them to school spending leads to a lot of open discussion. Assumptions about age play an intricate role in comparative interpretation and those assumptions are not always accurate. For example, a common assumption may be that people over 55 don't benefit directly from the public school system. This assumption wouldn't consider older parents or grandparents directly caring for children, or people over 55 that work in the school system (Henniker's largest full-time employer). Indirectly, well-funded school systems are known to increase property values, attract higher income residents, benefit local business, and boost a sense of community.


In review of this plot, we can see that from 2009 - 2024 the population of households likely to directly benefit from our public-school systems has shrunk from about 64% of the total population about 41% since 2009. The population that generally only benefits indirectly grew by that same 23% margin, from about 36% in 2009 to 59% in 2024. This could provide some rational to the long-term trend of allocating a smaller portion of property tax revenues to the public school system and spending a larger portion of funding on non-school services. For example, one rational may be that an older population has an increased need for ambulance services while fewer teachers may be needed to serve declining younger populations relative to the overall population.

Overall, this population by age comparison generates more questions than answers about the portion of funding allocated for school and non-school services over time. It doesn't provide concrete answers to questions due to assumptions being made and the complexity of how public schools interact with town culture and society. Nonetheless its interesting to consider the role of age in tax component allocation.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Key Findings & Insights

Overall, it appears that the 2024 Henniker tax rate is well positioned. Although the rate is high in comparison to NH as a whole, it is much more competitive when comparing it to towns of similar size and location. The Henniker property tax rate is very close to the average tax rate and below the median tax rate in our benchmark sample. Valuations in Henniker are noticeably higher in comparison to similar towns. I think this is a favorable position. Hennikers high property validation, combined with a competitive tax rate, gives it greater financial flexibility compared to similar towns. It can generate more revenue at a lower rate, maintaining a competitive edge while still funding services at or above benchmark levels. This also allows Henniker to reduce tax rates if desired, with less impact on service levels while keeping them aligned at or above comparable towns.

Over time Henniker tends to increase taxpayer burden at a faster rate than consumer inflation. However, wage inflation has largely cushioned that impact on taxpayers. Most recently, Henniker seems to have positioned its tax burden in-between consumer inflation and wage inflation. This is ideal since it allows for investment and growth in the community through increased tax funded services without depleting all taxpayer gains from wage inflation. As taxpayers disposable income increases, they are able to inject additional earnings into the Henniker community, creating a win-win situation.

Hennikers population appears to be aging and its impacting the public school system negatively while impacting town services positively. The percentage of revenue spent on the public school system as a portion of all taxes collected has largely declined since 1993. In that same time, town services have benefited from a larger portion of the property tax generated revenues. Although this trend is showing recent signs of easing, the long-term trend suggests that we should continue to expect weakening public schools and enhanced fire, police, highway, and other town services.
